How to use this tool

Step 1

Enter your SLA percentage

Type any value between 90% and 99.999% directly into the number field, or drag the slider to adjust it.

Step 2

Pick a common preset

Use the preset buttons to jump to widely-used SLA tiers: Two Nines (99%), Three Nines (99.9%), Four Nines (99.99%), or Five Nines (99.999%).

Step 3

Read the downtime budget

The table shows exactly how much downtime is "allowed" per year, month, week, and day before an SLA breach occurs.

Step 4

Use the color indicator

The large uptime display changes color — red for below 99.9%, amber for below 99.99%, and green for 99.99% and above.

Frequently Asked Questions

"Nines" refers to how many 9s appear in the uptime percentage. Two Nines = 99% uptime (allows ~87.6 h/year downtime). Three Nines = 99.9% (~8.77 h/year). Four Nines = 99.99% (~52.6 min/year). Five Nines = 99.999% (~5.26 min/year). Each additional nine reduces the allowed downtime by roughly a factor of 10.
The formula is: downtime = (1 − SLA/100) × period in seconds. Periods used are: year = 365.25 days, month = 30.44 days (average), week = 7 days, day = 24 hours. For example, at 99.9% SLA: (1 − 0.999) × 365.25 × 86400 ≈ 31,557 seconds ≈ 8 hours 45 minutes 57 seconds per year.
No. An SLA is a contractual commitment — a floor, not a ceiling. Cloud providers often achieve much higher actual uptime than their stated SLA. The SLA defines the threshold below which you are entitled to service credits or penalties. Always read the fine print: many SLAs exclude planned maintenance, force majeure, and issues caused by the customer.
For most production workloads, 99.9% (Three Nines, ~8.77 h/year) is the baseline. High-traffic e-commerce or financial applications typically require 99.99% (~52.6 min/year). Mission-critical systems that cannot tolerate even minutes of downtime annually aim for 99.999% (Five Nines). Remember: achieving higher nines requires redundant infrastructure (load balancers, multi-region, hot standbys) — not just a better SLA contract.
When your system depends on multiple independent services, the composite uptime is the product of all individual uptimes. For example, if Service A has 99.9% uptime and Service B has 99.9% uptime, your composite SLA is 99.9% × 99.9% = 99.8%. This means adding dependencies degrades overall availability — a strong argument for keeping architectures simple and using redundancy at each layer.
